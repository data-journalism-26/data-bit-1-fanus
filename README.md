# Layers of Oppression – Mental Health in Afghanistan

**Author:** Fanus Ghorjani
**Date:** 06 May 2026  

## Overview
This data bit examines the prevalence of mental health symptoms in Afghanistan and explores how psychological distress is distributed across different social groups.

Based on a survey of 2,698 participants, the visualization shows that symptoms of depression, anxiety and stress are widespread across the population. At the same time, the data reveals important differences: women, low-income populations, and rural communities report higher levels of distress.

The project argues that mental health in Afghanistan should not be understood as an individual or isolated issue, but as a collective condition shaped by structural factors.

---

## Key Insight
The central finding of this data bit is twofold:

- Mental distress is **widespread across all groups**  
- It becomes **more severe under conditions of inequality and insecurity**

This layered pattern suggests that psychological suffering is not randomly distributed, but linked to social and material conditions.

---

## Conceptual Approach
The analysis combines empirical data with a theoretical perspective inspired by Frantz Fanon. Fanon’s work on colonial violence highlights how prolonged exposure to domination, instability, and insecurity can shape not only social conditions, but also psychological experience. In this framework, mental health symptoms can be understood not only as individual disorders, but as responses to structural forms of violence. In this project, Fanon is used as an interpretive lens to understand how different forms of pressure (economic, social and political) accumulate and affect the psyche.

---

## Visualization
The chart presents three dimensions:

- **Exposure (social groups)**: income, gender, rural/urban context, and other factors  
- **Anxiety symptoms (%)**  
- **Depression symptoms (%)**

The visualization highlights both:
- the overall high level of distress across all groups  
- and the variation in intensity across different social layers  

---

## Data Source
- Neyazi et al. (2024)  
  *Afghanistan Mental Health Survey*  
  Sample size: n = 2,698  
  Measurement tool: DASS-21 (Depression, Anxiety, Stress Scales)

The percentages shown represent symptom prevalence within each group. Values are not causal and groups may overlap.
